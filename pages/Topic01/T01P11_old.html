Respiratory Rate and Effort
 
An infant in shock will have varying degrees of respiratory distress.
  • Tachypnea may be a sign that the infant is trying to compensate for a metabolic or mixed acidosis.
  • As shock worsens, work of breathing will increase.
  • In severe states of shock, the infant might have apneic episodes.
Gasping is an ominous sign of impending cardiorespiratory arrest.
O2 Saturation, Acid-Base Status, and Color
 
 
  • Evaluate the O2 saturation and blood gas to determine oxygenation and acid-base status.
  • Severe hypoxemia will worsen anaerobic metabolism.
  •  
    Pale or white skin color may indicate a low hemoglobin secondary to hemorrhage.
  • With severe anemia, O2 content of the blood will be low and will be an additional cause of tissue hypoxia.
Neurologic Status
 
  • Poor O2 delivery to the brain and accumulation of lactic acid will cause neurologic depression.
  • Neurologic signs include:
    • Irritability initially
    • Decreased level of consciousness
    • Hypotonia
    • Lethargy
In advanced states of shock, brain injury may lead to seizures.
Heart and Chest X-Ray
 
 
Supraventricular tachycardia
  • Heart murmurs are commonly heard in infants with and without congenital heart disease (CHD).
  • If CHD is suspected, obtain an echocardiogram.
  • An echocardiogram is also useful to assess heart function.
  • If the heart rhythm is abnormal, obtain an electrocardiogram (ECG).
 
Cardiomegaly in neonate with Truncus Arteriosus
 
 
 
 
 
 
  • Chest x-ray is helpful to evaluate
    • Heart size, shape, and location
    • Treatable causes of shock, such as pneumothorax
  • Enlarged heart size on x-ray:​
    • Myocardial dysfunction​
    • Congestive heart failure​
    • Congenital heart disease
  • Smaller than normal heart size:
    • Poor filling of the heart
    • Hyperexpanded lungs
Kidney Function
Urine Output
  • Urine output​ Indirectly reflects adequacy of kidney perfusion
  • Adequate blood flow and hydration necessary to maintain normal urine output​
Causes of Concern in Neonates​
  • Urine output less than 1 mL/kg/hour or declining urine output
  • Rising creatinine and blood urea nitrogen lab tests
