There is NO acute blood loss
  • 10 mL/kg normal saline (NS)
  • IV, UVC, or IO
  • Infusion time:
  • 30 minutes or more rapid for severe shock Repeat NS bolus as necessary
  • Positive response to treatment:
  • Less tachycardia
  • Improved CRT
  • Declining lactate
  • Improved pH
  • Improved BP
There IS acute blood loss
  • Packed red blood cells (PRBCs) While waiting for PRBCs, give 10 mL/kg NS
  • Infusion time:
  • PRBCs over 30 minutes to 2 hours
  • More rapid if severely anemic
  • Positive response to treatment:
  • Less tachycardia
  • Improved CRT
  • Declining lactate
  • Improved pH
  • Improved BP
  • Improved oxygenation

  • The treatment of obstructive shock relates to the obstructive etiology.
  • For a tension pneumothorax, treatment involves needle aspiration or chest tube insertion to evacuate the air.
  • For cardiac tamponade, treatment involves pericardiocentesis to remove air or fluid.
  • For ductal-dependent, left-outflow tract obstructive heart lesions, treatment involves initiation of a prostaglandin E1 infusion to establish and maintain patency of the ductus arteriosus.
 
 
 
 
Treatment of cardiogenic shock is aimed at correcting the underlying problems that negatively affect heart function.
The underlying problems include:
  • Hypoxia
  • Acidosis
  • Hypotension
  • Arrhythmias
  • Infection
  • Electrolyte and/or mineral imbalances
  • Hypoglycemia
  • Hypothermia
 
  • Treatment of septic shock involves a combination of the therapies used to treat hypovolemic and cardiogenic shock.
  • May require more fluid boluses because of capillary injury and leaking of fluid from the blood vessels into the interstitial space.
  • Dopamine infusion may be necessary.
  • Optimize oxygenation and ventilation.
